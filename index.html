Peeling back the layers: Unraveling the path to #1 NYT Bestselling Status for Fiction Novels¶

By: Nidhi Chilukuri

Introduction¶

The purpose behind this project is to walk you through the data science pipeline, with a focus on New York Times (NYT) Bestsellers and the traditional publishing industry. A lot of people ask the following question: why does it seem like every book is a NYT bestseller Well, according to the NYT itself, the bestseller list is determind solely by sales, during the previous weeks Sunday-to-Saturday sales period.

However, a book's sales are driven by a myriad of underlying factors including popularity, exposure, and marketing. Why are some books purchased more than others? Is it because they are objectively better? Is it because more people have talked about it on social media? Is it because their publishers are devoting more time, money, and energy into marketing?

Those questions lead us to more questions about how the publishing industry treats different books and their authors. As we'll see below, it's not a coincidence that many of the #1 NYT Bestselling authors are white. The traditional publishing industry is characterized by low racial diversity amongst both authors and novels. It's an exclusioanry, cutthroat industry that helps some books see sales numbers in the millions and others disappear off the face of the Earth as if they had never been published in the first place. The industry is dominated by The Big Five publishing houses, which control over 80 percent of the trade publishing market and therefore hold the most influence in book manufacturing and selling. To succeed in this brutal industry, connections and wealth are vital.

Together, we're going to dive into the New York Times #1 Bestsellers Lists to analyze the statistics of the books that earn the coveted honor of being placed on there. We already know that books get placed on the list due to sales. But we're going to peel back a few more layers and look into why these books are having such high sales in the first place. We'll be cross-referencing data from the NYT with Goodreads, and trying to see what the hidden factors are.

Let's get to it!

Goodreads is a book-tracking website owned by Amazon that has a lot of detailed information about millions of books. Sounds great, right? Part 1: Data Collection¶

The first step is to find data that is pertinent to our topic. Since we will be analyzing NYT Bestsellers, the best resource is the NYT itself. The website displays the top 10 books on the NYT Bestsellers list for Young Adult hardcover books on a weekly basis. For example, https://www.nytimes.com/books/best-sellers/2024/01/14/young-adult-hardcover/ displays the list for the week of January 14, 2024. We are interested in the #1 bestsellers for each week, which can also be found more easily here, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lists_of_The_New_York_Times_number-one_books. Both the NYT and the Wikipedia page contain the same data, and since the Wikipedia page is more consolidated with the specific data we need, we will pull our data from there instead. After obtaining the data from the Wikipedia page, we will extract more data for each book from Goodreads.

def get_book_urls(url):
    
    driver = webdriver.Chrome()
    driver.get(url)

    soup = BeautifulSoup(driver.page_source, "html.parser")
    tables = soup.find("table")

    book_urls = []

    for row in tables.find_all('tr'):
        cols = row.findAll('td')
        link = cols[1].find('a').get('href')
        book_urls.append(link)

    return book_urls

def individual_book_info(book_urls):
    
    driver = webdriver.Chrome()
    info = []

    for url in book_urls:
        driver.get("https://www.goodreads.com" + url)
        soup = BeautifulSoup(driver.page_source, "html.parser")

        b = soup.find("div")

        rating = 0.0
        book_title = ""
        reviews_count = 0
        ratings_count = 0
    
        # Get the rating from Goodreads
        for row in b.find_all('span', attrs={'aria-hidden': 'true'}):       
            aria_label = row.find('span')
            if aria_label:
                aria_label2 = aria_label.get('aria-label')
                rating = float(re.search(r'[\d,.]+', aria_label2[7:11]).group())

        # Get the title from Goodreads
        for row in b.find_all(lambda tag: tag.name == "div" and tag.get('class') == ["BookPageTitleSection__title"]):
            aria_label = row.find('h1')      
            aria_label2 = aria_label.get('aria-label')
            book_title = aria_label.get_text(strip=True) 

        # Get the number of ratings from Goodreads
        for row in b.find_all('span', attrs={'aria-hidden': 'true', 'data-testid': 'ratingsCount'}):
            ratings_text = row.get_text(strip=True)        
            ratings_number = re.search(r'[\d,]+', ratings_text).group()
            ratings_count = int(ratings_number.replace(',', ''))

        # Get the number of reviews from Goodreads
        for row in b.find_all('span', attrs={'aria-hidden': 'true', 'data-testid': 'reviewsCount'}):
            reviews_text = row.get_text(strip=True)
            reviews_number = re.search(r'[\d,]+', reviews_text).group()
            reviews_count = int(reviews_number.replace(',', ''))

        info.append([book_title, rating, ratings_count, reviews_count])
        
    return info

Part 4: Hypothesis Testing and Machine Learning¶

Now that we've visualized our data and seen how our three variables of interest, average rating, total ratings, and total reviews interact with each other, we need to take everything we've done and see if we can create a model that will predict whether a book is a #1 NYT bestseller based on its reviews and ratings on Goodreads. We will focus on the total ratings and total reviews, and leave average rating out of our model after seeing the graphs above.

We have two classes, Yes and No for whether a book is a #1 NYT bestseller. Since this is a classification problem, we won't be able to use Linear Regression. Instead, let's use a Logistic Regression model to classify books into two categories: 0 for No and 1 for Yes. Let's train our model and see how it performs. But first, we need to combine the two DataFrames we have (Non #1 NYT bestsellers and #1 NYT bestsellers) so that we can use them together to split into our training and testing sets.

Part 5: Interpretation¶

From our classification report, we can see that our precision and recall show that our model is fairly average at predicting whether a book will be a #1 NYT Bestseller based on its total ratings and total reviews on Goodreads. We wanted to understand how a book's popularity on a popular book-tracking site (Goodreads) affected its ability to become a #1 NYT Bestseller.

We learned that a book's popularity is just one factor that goes into whether it becomes a #1 NYT Bestseller. While using a book's total ratings and total reviews can be a useful indicator of its popularity and exposure, it doesn't necessarily indicate whether it will become a #1 NYT Bestseller. However, it does play a role, just as we suspected in the beginning about how popularity imapcts sales, and interactions on Goodreads can impact how well a book does. A book with a low number of reviews and ratings may not do as well as a book with more ratings and reviews. Exposure does impact how well a book does.

This was a baseline analysis of Goodreads and NYT Bestseller data. It's only the starting point, and there's so much more to explore! For the future, we could examine more years and other factors aside from average rating, total ratings, and total reviews on Goodreads. There's many potential factors that impact how well a book sells, and we could use those to improve our model and better understand how the NYT Bestsellers List operates.

While scraping data from Goodreads can be a challenge, we can also learn a lot about not just book statistics but also about the traditional publishing industry as a whole. Until we work to peel apart the layers that make up a successful book, we won't be able to dismantle the exclusionary practices of the industry. There's a lot of work to be done, and it starts here!
